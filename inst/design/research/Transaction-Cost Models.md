# Transaction-Cost Models for a Deterministic Event-Sourced R Backtesting Framework

## Literature foundations

The strongest constraint the literature places on a backtest cost API is not ÔÇťwhat costs exist,ÔÇŁ but **which frictions are naturally represented as a transformed execution price, which are naturally represented as an explicit fee cashflow, and which are not settled at fill time at all**. PeroldÔÇÖs implementation shortfall framework measures the difference between the paper portfolio at decision price and the implemented portfolio after execution; in the practitioner literature that decomposition expands into explicit costs, delay costs, market impact, and opportunity cost. Bloomberg BTCA, VirtuÔÇÖs TCA materials, and archived ITG documentation all keep benchmarking, implementation shortfall, venue comparison, and opportunity/risk tradeoffs in a **TCA/reporting layer**, not in a single fee formula. That distinction matters for ledgr: spread, slippage, commissions, and exchange/regulatory charges map cleanly to fill-time operations, but **delay cost and opportunity cost are benchmark concepts tied to order lifecycle and non-execution**, so they do not fit naturally inside a quantity-preserving `cost_model(fill_proposals, fill_context, params)` surface unless the cost layer starts pretending to be an OMS/TCA layer. ţłÇciteţłéturn8search0ţłéturn21view2ţłéturn10search3ţłéturn10search4ţłü

KissellÔÇÖs practitioner framework is especially useful because it treats transaction costs as **unbundled components** and then studies them across asset classes. The table of contents for *The Science of Algorithmic Trading and Portfolio Management* explicitly separates ÔÇťunbundled transaction cost components,ÔÇŁ ÔÇťtransaction cost classification,ÔÇŁ ÔÇťimplementation shortfall,ÔÇŁ and ÔÇťmarket impact models,ÔÇŁ while KissellÔÇÖs multi-asset work argues that cost analysis has to adapt to market structure differences rather than forcing one universal formula. For API design, that argues for a vocabulary of **small component models** rather than one opaque ÔÇťall-in costÔÇŁ toggle, and for an asset-aware registry or template mechanism rather than a giant asset-class enum trying to encode every fee schedule variant. ţłÇciteţłéturn9search14ţłéturn9search6ţłéturn9search15ţłü

Almgren and Chriss sit on the boundary between a **backtest cost interface** and an **execution-algorithm interface**. In SSRN and the related execution literature, their contribution is an optimal liquidation problem that trades off expected execution cost against execution risk and yields an optimal schedule; the same lineage motivates liquidity-adjusted VaR and formal execution trajectories. G├órleanu and PedersenÔÇÖs dynamic trading work pushes the same point from the portfolio side: with transaction costs, the optimal policy is to trade only partially toward a target, and to weight signals by their persistence. The API implication is that **impact models are not just additive fees**; once a cost depends on participation rate, volatility, execution horizon, and future trading path, the model starts to need order slicing, bar-volume consumption, or schedule control. A v1 cost API can still expose quantity-preserving price adjustments for simple impact proxies, but the literature warns that the fully rigorous descendants of AlmgrenÔÇôChriss belong closer to a future execution/liquidity layer than to a static fee calculator. ţłÇciteţłéturn21view0ţłéturn25search11ţłéturn25search13ţłéturn25search21ţłü

PedersenÔÇÖs *Efficiently Inefficient* is less useful for fee formulas than for **scope discipline**. In the bookÔÇÖs introduction, Pedersen frames exploitable inefficiency as bounded by infrastructure costs, trading frictions, and funding frictions; he points back to Brunnermeier and PedersenÔÇÖs market-liquidity/funding-liquidity linkage, where market liquidity and financing constraints reinforce one another in liquidity spirals. For ledgr, the constraint is conceptual: **funding and borrow costs are real, but they are not usually fill-time commissions**. They are stateful, calendar-driven, and often driven by financing conditions or inventory scarcity rather than by a specific trade row. That supports a clean accounting boundary in which borrow, margin interest, carry, and perpetual funding are handled as financing or periodic cashflow models, even if a given framework sometimes routes them through a ÔÇťcommissionÔÇŁ hook. ţłÇciteţłéturn25search0ţłéturn26search4ţłéturn26search7ţłü

HasbrouckÔÇÖs *Empirical Market Microstructure* is the best bridge from microstructure theory to API structure. In the preface and Part I roadmap, Hasbrouck places fixed transaction costs, RollÔÇÖs bid-ask spread estimator, sequential-trade models, continuous-auction models, and later price/trade models into a common arc: transaction price equals an efficient-price process plus microstructure noise, and signed trades matter because they carry information and inventory effects. That is precisely the boundary a backtest API needs. **Spread** is a price-noise/liquidity-access phenomenon; **impact** is a signed-trade/price-dynamics phenomenon; **information effects** come from order flow and adverse selection; and **efficient price** is unobservable. The API consequence is that a backtest-side cost model should not claim more than the data can support. With only next-bar OHLCV, one can model spread proxies, fixed slippage, basis-point slippage, and simple participation-based impact. One cannot honestly infer queue position, maker/taker status, or persistent information impact without stronger microstructure data or a separate execution simulator. ţłÇciteţłéturn23view0ţłéturn24view0ţłü

RollÔÇÖs estimator makes the same point in one line: observed serial covariance in transaction-price changes identifies the effective spread under restrictive assumptions. That is useful as a calibration idea for a spread proxy, but it is a **market-level estimator**, not a fill-time accounting rule. In other words: Roll is relevant to how a user chooses a spread parameter, not to how the cost hook should be shaped. GlostenÔÇôMilgrom and Kyle matter similarly. Their enduring relevance is to say **quoted spread, adverse-selection spread, and impact are different economic objects**. Their practical relevance to backtests is mostly as justification for keeping spread/slippage/impact as distinct model categories, not for embedding full market-making equilibrium logic in a v1 cost API. ţłÇciteţłéturn23view3ţłéturn23view0ţłü

The practitioner TCA stack reinforces this decomposition. Bloomberg BTCA explicitly markets multi-asset, full-life-cycle analysis with implementation shortfall, VWAP/TWAP, point-in-time and reversion/momentum benchmarks, while Virtu describes 100+ benchmarks, venue-specific cost analysis, and pre-/intra-/post-trade workflows. Those products do not collapse everything into a single commission number; they preserve benchmark semantics and analyze costs against market data across the trade lifecycle. For ledgr, that suggests an important negative constraint: the public cost API should record enough detailÔÇöfill price adjustments and explicit fee amountsÔÇöthat a later TCA/reporting layer can reconstruct implementation shortfall-style analytics, but the cost API itself need not become a full benchmark engine. ţłÇciteţłéturn21view2ţłéturn7search8ţłéturn10search3ţłü

A useful accounting decomposition for ledgr, consistent with the literature and the surveyed frameworks, is therefore: **price adjustments** for spread, slippage, and simple impact; **fee entries** for commissions, exchange/clearing/regulatory fees, rebates, and transaction taxes; **calendar or position cashflows** for borrow, carry, margin interest, and perpetual funding; and **TCA-only metrics** for delay cost, opportunity cost, and benchmark-relative shortfall. That decomposition is not a design choice invented from scratch; it is the common boundary across implementation-shortfall literature, microstructure theory, and modern backtesting engines. ţłÇciteţłéturn8search0ţłéturn23view0ţłéturn38view2ţłéturn40view1ţłü

Open design questions raised by the evidence: should ledgrÔÇÖs public vocabulary distinguish ÔÇťprice transformersÔÇŁ from ÔÇťfee addersÔÇŁ explicitly, or only by convention; should opportunity cost be preserved only for future OMS/TCA layers rather than named in the cost API at all; and if market impact enters v1, how minimal can that interface be without implying that ledgr already supports execution scheduling in the AlmgrenÔÇôChriss sense? ţłÇciteţłéturn21view0ţłéturn8search0ţłéturn21view2ţłü

The simpler frameworks are instructive precisely because of what they do **not** do. `bt` accepts a single commission function `fn(quantity, price)` and also lets users pass `bidoffer`, `cost_long`, and `cost_short` data frames into the strategy setup. Inside each large framework, the asset-model story also differs. Backtrader keeps a single commission object family and uses flags like `margin`, `commtype`, and `stocklike` to distinguish stock-like and futures-like behavior; named registrations can target data feeds, but the abstraction still feels legacy because the same class family is doing multiple jobs. ZiplineÔÇÖs public interface is deliberately narrowerÔÇöUS equities vs US futuresÔÇöwhile allowing futures commission and exchange fees to vary by root symbol, which is simple but not especially extensible across crypto/FX/options. NautilusTrader and LEAN both land on **object-per-venue / object-per-security** patterns, which better fit heterogeneous multi-asset portfolios. ţłÇciteţłéturn32view3ţłéturn35view0ţłéturn33view3ţłéturn37view0ţłéturn38view3ţłü

## Identity, serialization, and reproducibility

The surveyed competitor frameworks are surprisingly weak on **documented model identity**. LEAN, Zipline, NautilusTrader, backtrader, and the simpler Python libraries all document how to *assign* custom fee or slippage objects, but none of the cited docs describes a first-class, deterministic fingerprint of user-supplied model source for run identity. LEAN exposes `SetFeeModel`, `SetFillModel`, `SetSlippageModel`, and `SetMarginInterestRateModel` as per-security setters that accept interface objects or Python objects. Zipline tells users to subclass `CommissionModel` and `SlippageModel`. NautilusTrader is more configuration-oriented, especially for fill models via importable model paths, but still does not document a code fingerprint for arbitrary custom fee classes. For ledgr, that means there is no strong off-the-shelf precedent to borrow for ÔÇťhash a user callback into the run configÔÇŁ; the strongest precedent in the market is actually **to avoid free-form callbacks in the public surface, or at least not to promise that they are reproducibly fingerprinted**. ţłÇciteţłéturn38view4ţłéturn35view2ţłéturn36view4ţłéturn37view2ţłü

RÔÇÖs language model makes the problem harder, not easier. The R Language Definition is explicit that a closure contains the environment in which it was created as part of its definition, and later evaluation uses that closureÔÇÖs environment as enclosure. Base `environment()` documentation says the environment is associated with the function, and can be replaced. In practical terms, a seemingly innocent cost function can capture a parent environment full of mutable objects, package state, credentials, cached data, or large lookup tables. That is the fundamental trap: **hashing merely the source text of a closure is not enough if semantics depend on captured state; hashing the serialized closure may over-capture unstable or irrelevant state**. ţłÇciteţłéturn21view4ţłéturn22view3ţłéturn19search2ţłéturn19search6ţłéturn19search10ţłü

The low-level tools exist, but none is a silver bullet. `body()` exposes the function body, and `deparse()` / `deparse1()` turn expressions into strings. `serialize()` gives raw bytes; base R warns that while the interface is stable, the serialization format may change in future versions, so it is not a guaranteed long-term archival representation. The `digest` package hashes arbitrary R objects by serializing them internally, and even exposes a `serializeVersion` parameter. `codetools::findGlobals()` can approximate which globals a closure references, but its own docs say the result is only an approximation. Put together, the R-native options form a menu of imperfect choices: textual fingerprinting via `body()`/`deparse()`, object fingerprinting via `serialize()`/`digest()`, and closure-audit heuristics via `findGlobals()`. ţłÇciteţłéturn19search4ţłéturn19search5ţłéturn19search7ţłéturn18search6ţłéturn20search1ţłéturn20search5ţłéturn18search3ţłü

For ledgr specifically, the important distinction is between **fingerprinting a cost object** and **fingerprinting a free closure**. A classed cost object with an explicit constructor recordÔÇösay, type tag plus named scalar/vector parameters and possibly an ordered child listÔÇöhas a simple canonical form. A free closure does not. Even if ledgr captured the function body text, the same textual body could close over different environments; and even if ledgr serialized the closure, the resulting hash could become sensitive to non-semantic environment details, object ordering, external pointers, or version-specific serialization behavior. That is why the ÔÇťledgr-classed objects onlyÔÇŁ pattern buys so much: it narrows the configuration surface from arbitrary code to data-plus-known-constructor metadata. The cost is obvious too: it limits user extensibility in v1 and may force some advanced users into internal/experimental APIs until the fingerprinting story matures. ţłÇciteţłéturn21view4ţłéturn19search7ţłéturn20search1ţłéturn18search3ţłü

A chainable cost API sharpens the point. If `cost_model` is a single resolver function, the identity problem collapses to ÔÇťwhat exactly do you hash when the resolver is user code?ÔÇŁ If it is an **ordered list of classed transforms**, fingerprinting becomes straightforward in principle: hash the normalized list of step types, parameter payloads, and step order. The order itself matters and must be part of the digest. BacktraderÔÇÖs named commission object registrations, NautilusTraderÔÇÖs venue-level fee model objects, and LEANÔÇÖs per-security model objects all steer users toward assignable model instances precisely because such instances are easier to reason about than naked callbacks. None of them solves ledgrÔÇÖs reproducibility requirement completely, but all suggest that **public model identity should be object-based, not source-capture-first**. ţłÇciteţłéturn32view1ţłéturn37view0ţłéturn38view4ţłü

There is a second reproducibility issue beyond hashing: **persistence and replay semantics**. In a deterministic event-sourced engine, a cost model identity should ideally support ÔÇťsame config, same event logÔÇŁ across reruns. NautilusTraderÔÇÖs docs make that aspiration explicit for broader engine behavior: deterministic event ordering, shared research/live execution model, and deterministic simulated `TradeId` derivation in backtests. LEAN and Zipline are strongly configurable but do not, in the cited docs, discuss this identity problem in the same way. That makes ledgrÔÇÖs problem unusually sharp: the engineÔÇÖs architecture raises the bar from ÔÇťcan users override fee logic?ÔÇŁ to ÔÇťcan overrides be stamped into run identity in a stable, auditable way?ÔÇŁ ţłÇciteţłéturn36view3ţłéturn37view2ţłü

The highest-confidence design-space conclusion from the evidence is therefore narrow but clear: **if ledgr wants deterministic cost-model identity in v1, classed cost objects are far easier to justify than arbitrary user functions**. Source capture and closure inspection are plausible later, but only with explicit caveats about captured environments and R-version-sensitive serialization. ţłÇciteţłéturn22view3ţłéturn19search7ţłéturn20search1ţłü

Open design questions raised by the evidence: is ledgr willing to define cost identity only for classed objects in v1 and leave arbitrary closures ÔÇťunsupported for reproducible runsÔÇŁ; if closures are admitted later, is the authoritative fingerprint textual, serialized, or hybrid; and should ledgr reject closures that reference non-empty approximated globals, given that `findGlobals()` is only approximate? ţłÇciteţłéturn18search3ţłéturn19search7ţłéturn20search1ţłü

## Composability patterns

The competitor survey shows two stable composition patterns. The first, used by LEAN, Zipline, and NautilusTrader, is **separation by economic concern**: fees in one model, slippage/spread/impact in another, fills and quantity mutation in another, financing elsewhere again. The second, used by backtesting.py, `bt`, and often backtrader, is **single-surface composition**, where fees/spread/short-borrow/slippage are passed as constructor arguments or callbacks and the user is responsible for understanding ordering. LedgrÔÇÖs open design questionÔÇöchain vs single functionÔÇömaps directly onto that observed split. ţłÇciteţłéturn38view2ţłéturn38view3ţłéturn35view3ţłéturn36view1ţłéturn29view0ţłéturn30view3ţłü

The most important ordering fact is that **price adjustments and fee adders do not commute** if any fee depends on the adjusted notional or on the gross price after slippage. Zipline makes this separation explicit by generating transactions through slippage models and a separate commissions list in the blotter. LEAN says fill models determine price and quantity, can incorporate spread, and then work with slippage models, while fees are separate `IFeeModel`s on the security. Backtesting.py is a useful warning: its docs note that nonzero commission adjusts order prices and that spread is a separate entry-only knob, meaning the user is already living with implicit ordering decisions. For ledgr, the composability question is therefore not abstract; if the public API allows a chain, **the chain either needs explicit ordering semantics or it needs to restrict each stepÔÇÖs scope strongly enough that ordering is derived rather than free-form**. ţłÇciteţłéturn33view4ţłéturn38view2ţłéturn38view3ţłéturn29view0ţłéturn29view1ţłü

The common real combinations are more modest than the full literature might suggest. US retail equities often need spread + commission or ÔÇťzero commissionÔÇŁ plus spread + SEC/FINRA sell-side fees; US futures often need per-contract commission + exchange/clearing/NFA-type charges; crypto spot often needs maker/taker percent plus perhaps discounts or rebates; FX often needs spread plus overnight financing/carry; options often need per-contract plus exchange fees. Those combinations are one argument for a chain or composite object catalog. But they are also an argument **against** one monolithic custom function as the primary surface, because most users are not inventing entirely new economicsÔÇöthey are stacking a small family of known cost archetypes. ţłÇciteţłéturn5search0ţłéturn5search1ţłéturn5search2ţłéturn6search0ţłéturn6search1ţłéturn6search2ţłéturn27search7ţłü

What is commutative? Almost nothing beyond a narrow subset of additive fee terms. Per-trade fixed commission plus exchange fee plus regulatory fee is additive if all are independent of fill price and quantity except through already fixed quantities. Two price adjustmentsÔÇösay, fixed spread plus basis-point slippageÔÇöare not literally commutative once fees depend on resulting notional and once rounding or minimums matter, though they may be approximately interchangeable in toy models. ## Per-asset and stateful concerns

The survey makes clear that multi-asset realism is mostly implemented in one of three ways: **legacy flags**, **small asset buckets**, or **per-security / per-venue model assignment**. ## ledgr-specific open questions

On **API shape**, the external evidence points to two viable vocabularies rather than one. One vocabulary is **component constructors**: separate fee model, slippage model, fill model, financing model, and perhaps asset templates. LEAN, Zipline, and NautilusTrader all work this way in different idioms. The other vocabulary is **small convenience composites** for common user cases: backtesting.pyÔÇÖs `commission` and `spread`, vectorbtÔÇÖs broadcastable `fees`, and backtraderÔÇÖs commission-info objects. For ledgr, the design space is therefore not ÔÇťconstructor catalog vs no catalogÔÇŁ; it is ÔÇťhow many first-class named components does v1 expose before users have to drop into a composite or custom object?ÔÇŁ The narrowest evidence-based catalog that appears across frameworks is: explicit fee adder, spread/slippage price adjuster, per-contract/per-share fee variants, maker/taker fee variant, and a composition mechanism. ţłÇciteţłéturn38view3ţłéturn35view0ţłéturn36view4ţłéturn29view0ţłéturn28view0ţłü

On **user-function admission**, the research cuts against opening the door too widely in a reproducibility-first v1. The stronger frameworks all allow custom model objects, but they do not document deterministic hashing of arbitrary callback source. R closures make the fingerprinting problem sharper because environments are captured by definition. That means the most defensible design space for ledgr is: classed cost objects as the stable public contract; internal or experimental support for arbitrary functions later; or user functions admitted only with an explicit warning that they are not part of deterministic run identity unless wrapped in a classed object with canonical metadata. On **chain vs single function**, the market shows a deeper distinction than syntax.
