# Walk-Forward Evaluation for a Deterministic Event-Sourced Backtesting Framework

## Literature foundations

The literature relevant to an RFC for `ledgr` is narrower than the generic ŌĆ£what is walk-forward?ŌĆØ canon. The design-constraining pieces are about **how folds are defined**, **which dependencies between train and test invalidate estimates**, **what kinds of leakage purging and embargo actually address**, and **how model-selection over many trials changes the meaning of out-of-sample evidence**. In forecasting, TashmanŌĆÖs review is still the clearest taxonomy of out-of-sample test design: it distinguishes fixed versus rolling origins, rolling versus fixed windows, updating versus recalibration, and single versus multiple test periods, and treats these choices as protocol design variables rather than implementation details. In time-series ML, Bergmeir, Hyndman, and Koo narrow the circumstances in which ordinary cross-validation remains valid, showing that for **purely autoregressive** settings with uncorrelated errors, ordinary \(k\)-fold can be justified, but that result does **not** generalize to arbitrary financial feature sets or overlapping labels. For dependent-data CV more broadly, RacineŌĆÖs \(hv\)-block proposal is part of the design space, but later work notes that one of the intuitions used to motivate its consistency was flawed, so its theoretical status should be treated more cautiously than many practitioner summaries suggest. The cleanest way to relate walk-forward to time-series cross-validation is: **walk-forward is a family within time-series cross-validation, not a different idea**. `TimeSeriesSplit` in scikit-learn explicitly defines itself as a time-series cross-validator that produces temporally ordered splits, with growing training sets and optional gaps, while `rsample::rolling_origin()` and the newer `sliding_*()` family formalize the same idea for regular, irregular, and calendar-grouped indices. What finance adds is not a different notion of cross-validation, but stricter handling of **overlapping information sets**, **selection over many backtests**, and **strategy-search multiplicity**. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn30view0Ņłéturn18view0Ņłéturn18view1Ņłéturn44search0Ņłü

On ŌĆ£unbiasedness,ŌĆØ the literature is more conditional than many practitioner writeups suggest. Tashman treats out-of-sample testing as a protocol whose adequacy depends on whether the error-estimation design matches the operational forecasting design. Bergmeir et al. show a narrow positive result for standard CV in autoregressive settings with uncorrelated errors, which implicitly means that outside those conditions a chronology-preserving design is safer. L├│pez de PradoŌĆÖs purging/embargo framework does not establish a universal unbiasedness theorem for finance; it gives a **mechanism-correcting protocol** for cases where labels span forward intervals or where train examples overlap in information with test labels. For bootstrap methods, the asymptotic validity results depend on forms of weak dependence and stationarity; these are useful when the goal is inferential resampling, but they do not substitute for a deterministic walk-forward protocol tied to how the system would actually re-estimate parameters in production. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn44search0Ņłéturn44search4Ņłéturn44search10Ņłéturn33search6Ņłéturn9search1Ņłéturn37search1Ņłéturn37search5Ņłéturn37search13Ņłü

The most common look-ahead mechanisms in naive walk-forward implementations are mundane rather than exotic. Scikit-learnŌĆÖs own ŌĆ£common pitfallsŌĆØ chapter gives the general rule: never let test data participate in `fit`, including normalization, feature selection, PCA-like transforms, or imputation. In time-ordered settings, that means every feature-engineering object that estimates statistics must be fit **inside each training fold only**, then applied to the subsequent test fold. Finance adds another class of leakage: labels often depend on a future interval rather than the next timestamp, so simple chronological splits are not enough if a training sampleŌĆÖs information set overlaps the test label interval. That is exactly the problem purging and embargo are meant to correct. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn39view0Ņłéturn33search6Ņłéturn9search1Ņłü

**Pseudo-Mathematics and Financial Charlatanism** adds a different constraint: the walk-forward protocol is not honest if the *number of failed trials* is not part of the evidentiary picture. The paperŌĆÖs practical application shows how easy it is to manufacture impressive Sharpe ratios on random walks, and the conclusions argue that backtests that fail to report the number of trials attempted are materially incomplete, because selection itself is a biasing operator. **Harvey, Liu, and Zhu** add the ŌĆ£factor zooŌĆØ perspective: once a research process tries many hypotheses, conventional thresholds such as \(t \approx 2\) are too low. Their main design-space contribution is not a walk-forward protocol per se, but the requirement that any walk-forward result used as evidence should be paired with a family definition and a multiplicity correction regime. **The Deflated Sharpe Ratio** literature adds a parametric correction for two specific inflators: non-normal returns and multiple testing. The paper explicitly positions DSR as complementary to PBO: PBO is a nonparametric cross-validation diagnostic requiring rich backtest information, while DSR adjusts the significance threshold for Sharpe-based selection under multiplicity. It also ties directly to Minimum Track Record Length. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn41view2Ņłéturn42view2Ņłéturn43search2Ņłéturn43search4Ņłü

**Machine Learning for Asset Managers** compresses L├│pez de PradoŌĆÖs later synthesis into a more asset-management-oriented framing: cross-validation and Monte Carlo are presented as anti-overfitting tools, DSR is explicitly tied to false-discovery-style thinking, and the bookŌĆÖs core move is to push asset managers away from ŌĆ£strategy rule miningŌĆØ and toward hypothesis formation with explicit validation machinery. ## Reference implementations

Across the current ecosystem, ŌĆ£walk-forwardŌĆØ appears in three very different architectural forms. In **strategy backtesters**, it is usually a wrapper that repeatedly reuses the same execution engine on different data slices. In **ML/resampling toolkits**, it is a split object that emits train/test indices and leaves execution to the caller. In **optimization platforms**, it is a scheduler that periodically retrains or re-optimizes parameters inside an otherwise ordinary strategy run. This distinction matters for `ledgr`, because the choice determines whether folds are first-class execution artifacts or merely index metadata. ## Selection-integrity and overfitting machinery

Walk-forward by itself does not solve the research-selection problem. The central result of the PBO literature is precisely that, once many strategy variants are tried, the event of selecting the best backtest is already a biased operation. In the PBO paper, the core object is not a single train/test score but a matrix of strategy performance across many partitions, from which one can estimate how often the in-sample winner is mediocre out of sample. The paper explicitly criticizes hold-out as inadequate because it ignores the number of trials attempted. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn42view0Ņłéturn41view0Ņłü

| Technique | What it mathematically uses | What it returns | Most common implementation traps |
|---|---|---|---|
| PBO | Performance matrix of many candidate strategies over many CSCV partitions; compare the in-sample winnerŌĆÖs OOS rank, often through a logit transform of relative rank | Estimated probability the selected strategy is overfit | Treating one train/test split as sufficient; failing to define the candidate family; using too few partitions/candidates for stable estimates ŅłĆciteŅłéturn42view0Ņłéturn41view0Ņłü |
| CSCV | Symmetric combinatorial train/test partitions of the backtest path | Distribution of OOS rankings and performance degradation statistics | Confusing it with deployment walk-forward; mixing candidate-generation and evaluation stages; storing only stitched OOS instead of per-partition scores ŅłĆciteŅłéturn42view0Ņłü |
| CPCV | Sequential groups, many test-group combinations, plus purging/embargo by information intervals | Multiple backtest paths / pathwise OOS score distribution | Implementing purge logic incorrectly; forgetting that `gap` by row count is not the same as purging by label interval; equating CPCV paths with live trading schedules ŅłĆciteŅłéturn9search1Ņłéturn33search6Ņłéturn33search2Ņłü |
| DSR | Estimated Sharpe, sample length, skewness, kurtosis, and an estimate of the number of independent trials; builds on Probabilistic Sharpe Ratio | A Sharpe significance measure adjusted for non-normality and multiple testing | Using the raw count of tried parameter sets as if all were independent; ignoring non-normal moments; estimating trials after seeing results rather than from the search process definition ŅłĆciteŅłéturn41view2Ņłéturn42view2Ņłéturn43search2Ņłü |
| Holm / BH | Family of p-values across tested hypotheses | FWER- or FDR-controlled acceptance set | Failing to define the family; applying correction to post-selected survivors only; mixing dependent tests without acknowledging assumptions ŅłĆciteŅłéturn41view3Ņłéturn42view3Ņłü |
| Harvey-Liu-Zhu style multiple-testing penalty | Historical and current breadth of factor search; frequentist threshold adjustment | Recommended higher significance hurdles than the usual \(t \approx 2\) | Treating the paperŌĆÖs thresholds as universal constants rather than depending on hypothesis-breadth assumptions and research context ŅłĆciteŅłéturn41view3Ņłéturn42view3Ņłü |
| MinTRL | Observed Sharpe, target Sharpe threshold, confidence level, and return moments | Minimum track-record length needed before claiming significance | Using Gaussian approximations when returns are strongly non-normal; confusing minimum evidence length with expected profitable lifetime ŅłĆciteŅłéturn41view2Ņłéturn43search0Ņłéturn43search4Ņłü |

For RFC purposes, the design-space contribution of **PBO/CSCV** is that they require a **candidate-by-partition score matrix**. If a walk-forward feature only emits one stitched equity curve plus aggregate statistics, it may still be useful for operational realism, but it is insufficient input for the strongest selection-integrity diagnostics discussed in Bailey et al. ## Design patterns and anti-patterns

The recurring successful pattern across implementations is **per-fold selection followed by immediate forward test on the next unseen period**, with the selection object preserved as an artifact. `quantstrat` does this explicitly: optimize on the train timespan, choose one parameter combination with `obj.func`, then apply only that combination to the immediately following test timespan. `vectorbt`ŌĆÖs example notebook does the same using `idxmax()` per split. PortfolioAnalytics likewise re-estimates at each rebalance and carries those optimized weights forward into the next period. This is the textbook honest walk-forward workflow because the OOS slice is not allowed to affect the parameter choice for that slice. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn17view0Ņłéturn11view0Ņłéturn19view0Ņłü

By contrast, **sweep-then-walk-forward-the-winner** is the recurring anti-pattern. If the ŌĆ£winnerŌĆØ is chosen using the entire historical span and is then marched through folds for a nicer-looking report, the supposedly walk-forward OOS evidence is already contaminated by post-selection on future data. That is exactly the kind of hidden trial-count problem criticized in the PBO and Pseudo-Mathematics papers. The painful implication is that a stitched walk-forward chart of the final promoted strategy is only honest if the promoted strategy was chosen from evidence that excluded those later OOS folds. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn42view0Ņłéturn41view1Ņłéturn42view1Ņłü

The ambiguous middle ground is **walk-forward-then-sweep-per-fold**. In honest usage, this means each fold conducts its own train-period sweep and propagates only that foldŌĆÖs winner to the next OOS segment. In dishonest usage, it can mean inspecting all folds and then choosing a parameterization or selection recipe that retrospectively maximizes the combined walk-forward record. Public frameworks do not prevent this; they mainly differ in how much they surface the temptation. QuantConnectŌĆÖs optimization UI is a good example of comparatively mature reporting because it emphasizes **parameter stability regions**, not just the single best cell in a heatmap. That is an explicit acknowledgement that the ŌĆ£winnerŌĆØ can be defined by robustness rather than only by maximal train score. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn31view2Ņłéturn31view0Ņłü

On parameter selection, there are three recurring choices. The simplest is **argmax on a chosen train metric**; that is the public default in `quantstrat` and in the `vectorbt` notebook example. A more robust variation is **stability-region selection**, where a plateau of nearby parameter combinations that deliver similar objective values is preferred to a sharp spike; QuantConnectŌĆÖs docs discuss exactly this under ŌĆ£Parameter Stability.ŌĆØ A third option, common in research but less formalized in framework APIs, is to propagate a **top-N robust set** into diagnostic reporting instead of only the single winner. The literature does not supply a single canonical answer, but it does strongly imply that frameworks should preserve the data necessary to compare these choices later. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn17view0Ņłéturn11view0Ņłéturn31view2Ņłü

Reporting patterns also converge. The useful minimum is **per-fold OOS performance plus aggregate stitched OOS performance**. Better implementations or workflows add **train-versus-test gaps**, **parameter paths across folds**, and **sensitivity or stability surfaces**. LEANŌĆÖs optimization results page is unusually explicit here: it shows equity curves, parameter charts, and the full table of backtest results rather than only the best candidate. That makes it much easier to tell whether performance comes from a stable ridge or a fragile spike. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn31view2Ņłü

On persistence, the ecosystem splits between **fold definitions as separate artifacts** and **fold definitions as transient execution settings**. Resampling libraries like `rsample` and `TimeSeriesSplit` make the fold definition itself a reusable object. Walk-forward wrappers like `quantstrat::walk.forward()` internalize fold construction and mostly expose the results. Configuration-driven engines like NautilusTrader sit in between: `BacktestDataConfig` and `BacktestRunConfig` make slice boundaries explicit config, which can be persisted and hashed even though the framework does not market them as ŌĆ£fold objects.ŌĆØ That middle model is especially relevant to `ledgr`, because it can satisfy deterministic reproduction without inventing a second execution engine. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn18view0Ņłéturn18view1Ņłéturn30view0Ņłéturn27view1Ņłéturn28view0Ņłü

Determinism has two layers in the reference implementations. The first is **fold determinism**: boundaries, train/test sizes, and gaps must be explicit. The second is **execution determinism inside each fold**: same data ordering, same parameterization, same random seed, same numerical settings. Scikit-learnŌĆÖs docs warn that randomness in estimators and splitters can affect conclusions, while NautilusTrader explicitly advertises deterministic execution in timestamp order. For a framework like `ledgr`, seed placement therefore belongs in the walk-forward identity, not just the underlying backtest call. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn39view0Ņłéturn27view1Ņłéturn27view0Ņłü

On regime detection and market-state boundaries, the rigorous treatment in the mainstream literature is noticeably thinner than practitioner enthusiasm suggests. Tashman, rsample, `TimeSeriesSplit`, and most backtesting frameworks all define folds in calendar or index space. State-based segmentation can be sensible as a research design, but it creates two extra hazards: comparability across folds becomes murkier, and the regime classifier itself can leak if it is trained or calibrated with future information. None of the major public walk-forward APIs reviewed here makes market-state segmentation a first-class concept. That absence is itself a design signal: if `ledgr` ever supports state-defined folds, the fold definition will need to record not just boundaries but the status of the regime model used to derive them. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn44search0Ņłéturn18view1Ņłéturn30view0Ņłü

- Should the RFC define ŌĆ£honest OOS evidenceŌĆØ procedurally, in terms of the order of sweep, selection, and test propagation, rather than only in terms of data dates? The literature strongly suggests yes, but the exact rule belongs in the design. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn42view0Ņłéturn41view1Ņłü

## Ledgr-specific design questions

Your architecture is stricter than almost every public reference implementation because it adds **sealed, hashed snapshots** and an explicitly **append-only event/ledger worldview**. The walk-forward literature mostly assumes one time-indexed dataset partitioned into folds; the frameworks mostly slice one in-memory object, one bundle, or one catalog by date. `quantstrat` literally indexes one `symbol` object by train/test timespan. `rsample` and `TimeSeriesSplit` create split indices over one table or array. NautilusTrader can physically partition Parquet catalog data and slice by start/end time, but the slice definition is still runtime configuration, not a sealed immutable artifact identity. So the literature is largely silent on ŌĆ£test-set sealingŌĆØ; in that respect, `ledgr` is adding an invariant stronger than the field norm. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn17view0Ņłéturn18view1Ņłéturn30view0Ņłéturn28view0Ņłü

That creates a genuine design-space choice for **snapshot interaction**. One option is a **single sealed snapshot** whose identity is fixed once and whose walk-forward folds are date windows inside that snapshot. This matches the way most frameworks think: one bundle, one object, many train/test slices. The alternative is **multiple sealed fold-specific snapshots**. That would make each test fold independently sealable and hashable, but it would also move fold construction into the data-artifact layer and complicate promotion and reproducibility semantics. The literature does not force either shape. The closest external precedent is probably configuration-driven data slicing in NautilusTrader and bundle-driven single-run slicing in Zipline and LEAN. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28view0Ņłéturn25view1Ņłéturn31view1Ņłü

On **sweep interaction**, the strongest competitor evidence favors making walk-forward a **composition over the existing execution core**, not a peer engine. `quantstrat::walk.forward()` is the clearest example: it is just a wrapper around `apply.paramset()` and `applyStrategy()`. PortfolioAnalytics similarly wraps repeated `optimize.portfolio()` calls. Even LEANŌĆÖs walk-forward optimization is a scheduling layer on top of the same algorithm engine, not a separate backtester. That is highly compatible with your invariant that there must not be a second execution engine. The real design question is whether the public API looks like a walk-forward-specific wrapper around `ledgr_sweep`, or whether walk-forward consumes a generic fold object that can be passed into both `ledgr_run` and `ledgr_sweep`. The ecosystem supports both public surfaces. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn17view0Ņłéturn19view0Ņłéturn31view0Ņłéturn18view1Ņłéturn30view0Ņłü

On **promotion interaction**, there is little strong public precedent for ŌĆ£promoting a walk-forward resultŌĆØ as a durable production artifact. Public platforms usually promote either a **single candidate parameter set** or a **single algorithm code path**, not a schedule of different candidates by future period. Quantstrat saves audit artifacts and a concatenated testing portfolio/orderbook, but that is still backward-looking analysis. LEAN stores optimization jobs, all executed backtests, and parameter-result surfaces, but the user still chooses what to do next. So for `ledgr`, the core questions are architectural rather than conventional: does promotion mean promoting one globally selected candidate after walk-forward evidence, promoting the **per-fold parameter path** as a schedule artifact, or promoting a higher-level selection rule that will choose future parameters when new data arrive? The references map the options, but they do not resolve them. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn17view0Ņłéturn31view1Ņłéturn31view2Ņłü

On **OMS interaction**, the upcoming order-event stream is more natural than strained if folds are treated as explicit runs. Backtrader, Zipline, and NautilusTrader all already think in terms of orders, fills, positions, and ledgers/reports per run. NautilusTraderŌĆÖs `ReportProvider` turns raw trading data into structured orders and fills reports, and Zipline exposes a `Ledger` object tracking orders, transactions, and portfolio state. The pressure point is not conceptual fit; it is artifact multiplication. Once every fold can emit its own order-event stream, a walk-forward session becomes a collection of correlated but distinct event logs, and the promotion/persistence layer needs to decide whether to store them individually, stitch them, or reference them indirectly. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn27view3Ņłéturn24view0Ņłéturn20view1Ņłü

The **reproducibility hash** for a walk-forward run is not settled by the literature, but the references imply the components that matter. Because all reviewed systems make fold boundaries, train/test sizes, and parameter definitions decisive to results, a walk-forward identity should at least bind: the data artifact identity, fold-definition identity, candidate grid identity, objective/selection-rule identity, execution version identity, and any random seeds that affect either estimation or scoring. That is not a recommendation derived from one paper; it is an inference from how the ecosystems expose reproducible configs and how the integrity literature insists that trial counts and selection rules are part of the evidence. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn27view1Ņłéturn31view1Ņłéturn42view0Ņłéturn41view1Ņłü

The **public API shape** in the current ecosystem falls into three families. The first is **monolithic wrapper**: `quantstrat::walk.forward()`. The second is **fold-constructor plus downstream engine**: `rsample` and `TimeSeriesSplit`. The third is **configuration object plus batch runner**: NautilusTraderŌĆÖs `BacktestRunConfig`/`BacktestNode`. For `ledgr`, the main question is which family best respects sealed snapshots and durable promotion artifacts. Because snapshots and event logs are already first-class in your design, the config-object family is arguably the most composition-friendly reference point, but the wrapper model is closer to how users think about walk-forward in quantstrat and LEAN. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn17view0Ņłéturn18view1Ņłéturn30view0Ņłéturn27view1Ņłü

The strongest single influence to study before drafting the RFC seed is **NautilusTrader**.
